It's OK to Be Seen Trying

4 min read✦Track & Field

I've been thinking about how a race can do this strange thing to your brain. How your body can be warmed up, loose, steady, ready to go, and your mind still finds a way to unravel everything. Especially in the 400 hurdles. Especially when you care too much.

Before my race I was okay. I stretched, shook my legs out, did those little half-hearted skips down the straightaway that are supposed to trick your nerves into thinking you’re calm. I was telling myself all the usual mantras. Trust your rhythm. Stay tall. Breathe between hurdles. It all felt manageable. I was holding it together.

The logic goes something like: if people see you trying and you still lose, you've lost twice. You've lost the race and you've lost the protection of "I wasn't even really going for it." The effort becomes the liability.

But then I started thinking."

And it wasn’t even dramatic at first. Just small, creeping thoughts that cracked their way into my head. What if your steps are off? What if you hit hurdle three again? What if your body doesn’t listen to you today?.

And I know better, I do. I know that the minute you overthink this race, it owns you. But knowing doesn’t always save you.

The official called us to the line. I crouched into my blocks, but mentally I was floating somewhere ten meters ahead, watching the version of me that screws it all up.

The gun goes off. My legs move, but they feel… distant. Heavy, like I'm chasing my own body around the track. Hurdle one is clunky. Hurdle two, I hesitate. My steps are off and my brain’s louder than my breathing now.

I keep thinking about how this race isn’t supposed to feel like this. How I've been training, how I've been working, how I wanted today to be the day I finally trusted myself. But instead it’s just me fighting my own mind for 400 meters, tripping over invisible fears as much as real hurdles.

The backstretch hits and I'm falling apart quietly. It's not always visible from the outside, the way doubt curls up in your chest, the way it makes your legs forget how to drive, your arms forget how to pump, your lungs forget how to open.

By the curve I'm drowning. lactic acid screaming, sure, but it’s the mental noise that’s worse. you’re blowing it. they’re catching you. you’re not enough today.

And still, I finish. I force my way through the last two hurdles, cross the line, try to hold my face together so no one sees how much I cracked before I ever really raced.

And afterward I just stood there, breathing too hard, staring at the track like it betrayed me when really, it was my mind.

Here's the part I don't say out loud: I love this event. I love the rhythm, the grit, the quiet toughness it demands. I love the version of me I want to be when I run it: the one who trusts her stride, who doesn’t flinch when it gets hard, who keeps her head clear. But the truth is, some days you can love something and still completely fall apart inside it.

We don’t talk enough about the frustration of that. How exhausting it is to care, to crave progress, to visualize better versions of yourself and still lose to your own head mid-race.

People love to say “it’s all mental” like that makes it easier. But honestly, that’s what makes it harder. You can’t ice-bath your brain. There's no foam roller for doubt.

So maybe the real work isn’t pretending the doubt goes away. Maybe it’s figuring out how to run with it. How to stand there nervous and unsure and still give yourself a shot. Because the thinking never fully stops. The overthinking, the second-guessing, the tiny voice saying maybe you can’t, it shows up.

But so do you.

I was fine. Then I started thinking. and maybe next time, i’ll think too much again. But maybe next time, I won't let that be the reason I fall apart.
