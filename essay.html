It's OK to Be Seen Trying

4 min read✦Track & Field

There's a kind of athlete who shows up to every practice, every race, every lift — and works incredibly hard to make it look like they aren't really trying. Like it just happens. Like the effort is casual.

You know this person. You might be this person.

The logic goes something like: if people see you trying and you still lose, you've lost twice. You've lost the race and you've lost the protection of "I wasn't even really going for it." The effort becomes the liability.

"The effort becomes the liability. What a thing to believe about yourself."

But here's what actually happens when you hide your effort: you stop trusting yourself. You build a version of competition where your real performance — the one where you actually care — never gets tested. And an untested thing stays small forever.

What you're actually afraid of

It's not failure. Failure is fine. You've failed before. You'll fail again and it's survivable.

What you're afraid of is being seen wanting something and not getting it. The visibility of the wanting. That specific exposure — I cared about this, I went for it, and it wasn't enough — feels unbearable in a way that quiet failure doesn't.

But the quiet failure is the one that costs you. Not the race. The habit of protecting yourself from your own ambition.

The people who run their best races run them like the outcome is the only thing that matters. Not because they're reckless. Because they've decided that trying — fully, visibly, without the insurance policy of seeming indifferent — is worth the risk.

It is. It always is. Go be seen trying.
